Kernel Teachings of the Buddha

The Four Noble Truths

The Buddha began his teaching with the Four Noble Truths, which form the foundation of the path:

  1. The Truth of Unsatisfactoriness (Dukkha): All conditioned existence is marked by unsatisfactoriness. Birth, aging, illness, death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, and despair are unsatisfactory. Even pleasure is unsatisfactory because it is impermanent and unreliable.
  2. The Truth of the Origin of Unsatisfactoriness: Craving (taṇhā) is the cause. Craving for sensual pleasures, craving for existence, and craving for non-existence drive the cycle of rebirth and perpetuate unsatisfactoriness.
  3. The Truth of the Cessation of Unsatisfactoriness: When craving is abandoned, unsatisfactoriness ceases. This cessation is Nibbāna, the unconditioned state beyond birth and death.
  4. The Truth of the Path: The Noble Eightfold Path leads to cessation. It consists of:
    • Right View
    • Right Intention
    • Right Speech
    • Right Action
    • Right Livelihood
    • Right Effort: Cultivating wholesome states, abandoning unwholesome ones.
    • Right Mindfulness: Clear awareness of body, feelings, mind, and phenomena.
    • Right Concentration: Developing meditative absorption (jhāna).

The Five Aggregates

The Buddha analyzed the human being into five aggregates (khandhas), which together constitute what we call “a person”:

  1. Form (Rūpa): The physical body and material phenomena.
  2. Feeling (Vedanā): Pleasant, painful, or neutral sensations.
  3. Perception (Saññā): Recognition and labeling of experiences.
  4. Fabrications (Saṅkhārā): Volitional activities, mental formations, intentions.
  5. Consciousness (Viññāṇa): Awareness of sensory and mental objects.

The Buddha taught that none of these aggregates are self. They are impermanent, unsatisfactory, and not to be regarded as “I” or “mine.” In SN 22.59 (Anattā-lakkhaṇa Sutta), he instructed disciples to see each aggregate as: “This is not mine, this I am not, this is not my self.”

Consciousness and Name-Form Loop

In dependent origination (SN 12.2), consciousness and name-form (nāma-rūpa) condition each other. Consciousness requires name-form to manifest, and name-form requires consciousness to function. This mutual dependence shows that there is no independent self, only conditioned processes arising and ceasing. The Buddha emphasized that seeing this loop clearly dismantles the illusion of a permanent “I.”

Kamma: The Law of Intention

Kamma (Karma) is not “fate” or “cosmic justice.” It is specifically defined as Intention (Cetanā). The Buddha redefined Kamma from the ritual action of his time to the mental action of the present. In the Nibbedhika Sutta (AN 6.63), he states: “Intention, I tell you, is Kamma.” It is the “driving force” within the loop of Name-and-Form.

  • Intention as the Seed: Every volitional act—whether through thought, speech, or body—is a seed planted in the field of consciousness.
  • The Result (Vipāka): Kamma is the action; Vipāka is the result. Results are not “punishments” but the natural ripening of the quality of the original intention.
  • The “Loop” Connection: Kamma is the Fabrication (Saṅkhāra) that keeps the loop of consciousness spinning. Unskillful intentions (rooted in greed, hatred, or delusion) create “heavy” snapshots that lead to further bondage. Skillful intentions (rooted in non-attachment, kindness, and wisdom) “lighten” the loop.
  • The End of Kamma: The ultimate goal of the Path is not “good Kamma,” but the Cessation of Kamma. By acting with pure presence and no grasping (Vipassanā), the practitioner stops planting new seeds, eventually causing the entire cycle to run out of fuel.

Anattā (Not-Self)

The Buddha’s radical insight was that there is no permanent, unchanging self. The aggregates are conditioned, impermanent, and unsatisfactory. Clinging to them as “I” or “mine” creates the illusion of self and perpetuates unsatisfactoriness. Seeing clearly that all phenomena are not-self leads to disenchantment (nibbidā), dispassion (virāga), and liberation (nibbāna).

The Method of Observation

In the Mahāsatipaṭṭhāna Sutta (DN 22), the Buddha provides the protocol for deconstructing the illusion of self by observing the flow of mental and physical events in three ways:

  • Arising: Seeing the conditions that bring an event into existence.
  • Vanishing: Seeing the event dissolve immediately upon arising.
  • Arising and Vanishing: Seeing the high-speed, intermittent nature of the entire process.

This is done by abiding "independent, not clinging to anything in the world." This is the functional application of Virāga (dispassion) in real-time. By observing the "flicker" rather than the "form," the mind realizes there is no solid core to hold onto. This leads systematically to disenchantment (nibbidā), dispassion (virāga), and liberation (nibbāna).

The Natural Flow of Awakening

The Buddha described awakening not as something forced by sheer willpower, but as the natural result of providing the right conditions.

  • AN 11.2 (Cetanākaraṇīya Sutta): If one factor is present, the next arises naturally by law (dhammatā). Virtue leads to lack of remorse, which leads to joy, which leads to stillness, which leads to concentration.
  • SN 12.23 (Upanisa Sutta): The Buddha used the simile of rain flowing down a mountain into streams, ponds, lakes, and finally the ocean. In the same way, mindfulness and investigation naturally flow toward liberation.
  • Grasping as the Dam: Clinging (upādāna) blocks this natural flow. When grasping is abandoned, the mind moves toward peace without obstruction.
  • Practical Insight: Noticing the aggregates as impermanent and not-self weakens the dam of identity. The mind then flows naturally into concentration and insight.

The Four Jhānas

The Buddha taught the practice of concentration leading to four stages of meditative absorption (jhāna). These are not ends in themselves but powerful supports for insight:

  1. First Jhāna: Seclusion from sensuality and unwholesome states. Characterized by applied and sustained thought, rapture, and pleasure born of seclusion.
  2. Second Jhāna: With the calming of applied and sustained thought, rapture and pleasure remain, accompanied by inner stillness and unification of mind.
  3. Third Jhāna: Rapture fades, leaving equanimity and mindfulness, with pleasure still present.
  4. Fourth Jhāna: Pure equanimity and mindfulness, with neither pleasure nor pain.

Importance for Insight

Jhāna stabilizes the mind, making it clear and concentrated. This stability allows insight into impermanence, unsatisfactoriness, and not-self. While insight can arise without deep absorption, the jhānas provide a powerful foundation for penetrating the nature of reality and achieving liberation. The Buddha consistently emphasized that concentration and insight work together: concentration steadies the mind, insight liberates it.

The Removal of the Fetters (Saṃyojana)

Liberation is the systematic severing of the ten fetters that bind consciousness to the cycle of rebirth:

  1. Identity View (Sakkāya-diṭṭhi): The misconception that any of the five aggregates constitute a permanent "self."
  2. Doubt (Vicikicchā): Uncertainty regarding the Buddha’s map of reality and the effectiveness of the path.
  3. Attachment to Rites and Rituals (Sīlabbata-parāmāsa): The belief that mere external ceremonies or rules alone can lead to awakening.
  4. Sensual Desire (Kāmacchanda): The compulsive drive for pleasure through the six senses.
  5. Ill Will (Byāpāda): The mental reaction of aversion, resentment, or hostility.
  6. Craving for Form-Existence (Rūpa-rāga): Attachment to refined, material states of being (such as the first four Jhānas).
  7. Craving for Formless-Existence (Arūpa-rāga): Attachment to the subtle, non-physical states of consciousness.
  8. Conceit (Māna): The deep-seated, reflexive "I am" comparison—the subtle "scent" of self.
  9. Restlessness (Uddhacca): The final, subtle agitation or "flicker" within the mind.
  10. Ignorance (Avijjā): The fundamental failure to see the Four Noble Truths in every moment.

The Stages of Release

The path deconstructs these fetters in a specific, irreversible sequence:

  • Stream-entry (Sotāpatti): Completely severs the first three fetters. The illusion of a solid "I" is shattered.
  • Once-return (Sakadāgāmitā): Significantly weakens the 4th and 5th fetters (sensual desire and ill will).
  • Non-return (Anāgāmitā): Fully eliminates the 4th and 5th fetters; the mind is no longer capable of being pulled by lust or pushed by anger.
  • Arahantship: Eradicates the remaining five "higher" fetters (6–10). The root of ignorance is pulled up, and the cycle of rebirth ceases permanently.

Conclusion

Together, these teachings form a coherent path: understanding unsatisfactoriness, seeing through the aggregates, realizing not-self, and cultivating concentration and insight to reach Nibbāna.
